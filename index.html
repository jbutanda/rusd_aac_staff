For Special Education Staff

The team around a student is the intervention.

You already know what AAC is. You already work with students who use devices every day. The goal of this page is not to introduce. It is to sharpen. To make sure that every adult on your team, from the lead teacher to the 1:1 aide, is pulling in the same direction for the students who need a voice.

Audience ESN & MMSN Teachers and Aides
Presenter Jerry Butanda, M.S.-SLP
Role Speech-Language Pathologist, RUSD
Why This Page Exists

Not an introduction. A sharpening.

In specialized classrooms, the question is rarely "what is AAC." The question is "why is it not working the way we hoped," or "how do I get my team pulling together," or "we have been at this for years, now what." This page is for those questions.

The evidence on AAC has been clear for years. Communication is a right, not a privilege earned through readiness. What the evidence also shows, and what does not get enough attention, is that the team around a student is the single most important variable in whether that right becomes a lived experience. Not the device. Not the vocabulary set. The adults.

Consistency across that team is the lever. Frequency of modeling, shared expectations, clear communication about what is working and what is not. Those are the things that move students. The resources below are the practical pieces of that work.

Go Deeper

External resources worth your time.

These are the sources worth returning to when you have time for deeper reading. All are free, all are appropriate for specialized settings, and several are worth sharing directly with your aides and paraprofessionals.

University of North Carolina

Project Core

Free, evidence-based professional development modules and universal core vocabulary boards designed for school implementation. Written for classroom teams, not just specialists.

→
Blog & Practitioner Resources

PrAACtical AAC

A long-running, practitioner-friendly blog with short posts on almost any AAC topic you can think of. Searchable and consistently useful for day-to-day classroom questions.

→
AssistiveWare

Core Word Classroom & Learn AAC

Free printable core boards, short instructional videos, and implementation guides aimed squarely at teachers and support staff. Very digestible, no background required.

→
CommunicationFIRST

LISTEN & Advocacy Resources

A disability-rights organization led by AAC users. Their seven minute short film LISTEN is one of the most effective introductions to presuming competence that exists.

→
National Joint Committee · ASHA

Communication Bill of Rights

The fifteen fundamental rights that frame everything we do in AAC. One page, worth reading once a year, worth posting in every classroom that serves a student who uses AAC.

Contact

You are not on your own.

Start with the Speech-Language Pathologist at your school. They are your first point of contact for device issues, student-specific questions, and day-to-day coaching. If your site SLP loops me in or you have a question that falls outside their scope, feel free to reach out directly.
